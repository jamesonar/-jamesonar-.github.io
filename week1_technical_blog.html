Bear with me here...

Date: 5/6/2014

I'll discuss two of my three websites together because I like them for the same reason. This websites are Apple.com and Uber.com. If design thinking places and emphasis on empathy, creativity, and rationality, these two sites put the focus on empathy. The first thing that you'll likely notice on both of these sites is that they feature very prominent images on the top of their page.

The images practically take up the whole screen. I imagine the purpose of this is create an impression on the user before they do anything else. Apple's images generally features close up, high resolution photos of their products. This gives a strong impression regarding the quality of their product's design. In turn, Uber's site features a series of equally large photos of attractive, fashionable people on the move. This is Uber's attempt to define their service not by the cars themselfs, but by the people who use it.

The balance that both sites need to manage is the tradetrade-off between focusing on the impression versus the "rational" component of the site (i.e. getting the customer to do what you want them to). The sites take different approaches to this. Uber prominently displays a small sign up button in middle of their landing page while Apple, rightly assuming that prospective buyers of their products will need more information before they buy anything, requires that customers navigate through less conspicuous buttons along the top

The New York Times website is compeltely different from the first two, which is driven by function. The websites of Apple and Uber are designed as tools to market a product. The New York Times's site to a large extent is its product, and it's product is content. As a result, the website attemtps to display as much content as possible without feeling cluttered. Here rationality takes presedence over empathy.
